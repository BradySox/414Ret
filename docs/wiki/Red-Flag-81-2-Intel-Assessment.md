# PART 1 — INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT: RED FLAG, 1981

**Tactical Air Command — 414th Combat Training Squadron heritage brief**
**Subject:** the exercise, the Red Force, and the range threat array — Red Flag 81-2

## Bottom Line Up Front

Red Flag exists because Vietnam killed too many new crews. The **Red Baron** analyses found that
a fighter crew's survival odds improved dramatically after their **first ten combat missions** —
so Col. Richard **"Moody" Suter** sold TAC commander Gen. Robert J. **Dixon** on flying those ten
missions in peacetime, against a real, thinking enemy, over instrumented ranges. Dixon approved
in **May 1975**; the first Red Flag flew **29 November 1975** (37 aircraft, 561 personnel, 552
sorties, the **49th TFW's F-4Ds** as the primary unit); the **4440th Tactical Fighter Training
Group (Red Flag)** was chartered **1 March 1976**. By 1981 the flag was the finishing school of
the tactical air force — and the modern exercise is run by the real-world **414th Combat Training
Squadron**, a designation this squadron did not pick by accident.

**The premise of this campaign:** you don't fly *at* Red Flag 81-2 — you fly **the war Red Flag
81-2 was simulating**, from inside the simulation. Every threat below was really on those ranges,
built to kill you on paper so the real thing couldn't.

## Section I — The F-4 and the flag

The Phantom was the backbone TAC fighter of Red Flag's first decade:

- **Red Flag I (Nov–Dec 1975):** 49th TFW F-4Ds, with OV-10 FACs, F-105 Wild Weasels and CH-53s
  in support. Before Red Flag, crews trained "F-4 versus F-4" — the whole point was to stop that.
- **The regulars:** the **4th TFW (Seymour Johnson) F-4Es** — the 81-2 player unit; the **347th
  TFW F-4Es** (photographed on the Nellis ramp in 1981); the **388th TFW F-4Es** until their 1980
  F-16 conversion; **George AFB's F-4G Advanced Wild Weasels**; **RF-4C** squadrons flying the
  recon block of nearly every flag; ANG and Reserve F-4C/Ds.
- **Red Flag 81-2:** the second flag of FY81 — the **336th TFS "Rocketeers"** (4th TFW, Seymour
  Johnson AFB) on a two-week detachment to Nellis. The commercial campaign this build is styled
  after recreates that detachment across **13 missions**; this campaign's ~13-turn arc mirrors it.

## Section II — The Red Force (air)

- **The Aggressors:** the **64th and 65th Aggressor Squadrons** at Nellis flew **F-5E Tiger IIs**
  simulating the MiG-21 — hand-picked pilots schooled in Soviet/NVA GCI doctrine (the enterprise
  began as the 64th FWS in fall 1972, first on T-38s). Small, hard to see, guns-and-heaters, and
  flown better than you: **the airframe is their only handicap.** Their doctrine is the ambush —
  GCI-vectored, late, one slashing pass through the package near its target, then out.
- **Constant Peg — the real MiGs:** from **July 1979** the **4477th Test & Evaluation Squadron
  "Red Eagles"** flew *actual* **MiG-17s and MiG-21s** (later MiG-23s) out of the **Tonopah Test
  Range airfield** — exposing crews to the real airplane, not a simulation of it. Red MiGs
  operating from TTR in this campaign is the literal 1981 order of battle. The program was
  deep-black in 1981; the campaign's **Groom Lake Box** sanctuary carries the same "you never saw
  this" energy.

## Section III — The Red Force (ground & the array)

- **The threat array:** **SA-2 and SA-3 emulator sites** with real emitter behavior, **"Smoky
  SAM"** (GTR-18) visual launch simulators, **ZSU-23-4 and AAA gun simulators**, and GCI/EW
  emitters concentrated around the **Tolicha Peak Electronic Combat Range** (between Tonopah and
  Beatty) and the north ranges. The Red Team historically set up on the **west side** of the
  Nellis range complex — exactly where this campaign puts it.
- **The targets:** 50+ types across ranges 61–76 — a **mock airfield** dressed with mothballed
  airframes at Tolicha Peak, **convoys** (some moving), **tank arrays** (Kawich Valley / Gold
  Flat in the campaign), trains, industrial complexes, POL, bridges, radar/SAM/AAA sites.
- **The Box:** the **Groom Lake restricted airspace** sat inside the range complex and was
  **never** released to exercise traffic. Enter it and your flag was over. In the campaign it
  never releases either — violations bleed the assessment (Political Will) instead of your wings.

## Section IV — Friendly forces, January 1981

The 336th's F-4E detachment at Nellis with its supporting cast: a second F-4E squadron flying
MiGCAP/escort, **A-10As** (new to the flag from ~1979 — tank-killers learning the FEBA), **OV-10
FACs** (at the flag since Red Flag I), an **RF-101B** photo bird standing in for the RF-4C recon
block, **E-3A** AWACS (operational 1977), the **KC-135 boom bridge**, and **SAC B-52 cells**
flying Arc Light boxes from off-station — SAC participated from the earliest flags. Helo lift at
**Indian Springs AAF** (Creech's 1981 name) and a toe-hold at **Camp Mercury** on the Nevada Test
Site boundary complete the laydown.

## Section V — Courses of action

**MOST LIKELY (the exercise script):** the Red Force defends the array in depth — GCI ambushes on
your strike packages, the emulator rings covering the corridors, the ground force pressing the
FEBA on schedule (the Week Two counterattack), logistics flowing down the Tonopah–TTR–Pahute web.

**MOST DANGEROUS:** you fly it like a peacetime exercise. The aggressors are scored at **High**
skill, the array is real, and the assessment meters don't care that it's "training" — a sloppy
week loses this campaign exactly the way ten sloppy missions lost crews over Hanoi.

  • They scramble LATE and merge CLOSE to your target — expect the slash at the IP.

# PART 3 — FIVE-MINUTE SPOKEN BRIEF *(read-aloud script)*

> *Briefer's note: ~500 words. The voice is the exercise's own premise — brief it as the war.*

Gentlemen. Welcome to Nellis. Take a seat.

Somewhere over North Vietnam, between 1965 and 1973, we learned a number: ten. Survive your first
ten combat missions and your odds got dramatically better. Too many of our people didn't get ten.
So the Air Force built this place — a war you can lose without dying — and called it Red Flag.
Understand what that means: **everything out on those ranges tonight was put there to kill you on
paper, so the real thing never gets the chance.** Fly it like it's real, because the version of
you that doesn't will fly the real thing unprepared.

Here's your enemy. The **64th and 65th Aggressors** — F-5s painted like the threat, flown by
hand-picked instructors who fly MiG tactics better than the MiG pilots do. They will not meet you
head-on and fair. They'll sit low behind the ridges under GCI, let you push deep, and hit your
strikers one time, hard, right at the target — then they'll be gone before you turn around. And
north of them, out of Tonopah Test Range — **real MiGs.** You didn't hear that. You'll also never
overfly the block of airspace around Groom Lake. Not once. Not chasing a kill, not cutting a
corner home. **The Box is closed.** It stays closed all three weeks, and every violation gets
briefed to people whose signatures matter.

The ground threat is the array: **SA-2 and SA-3 sites around Tolicha Peak and the Test Range that
behave exactly like the ones over Hanoi**, gun belts on every corridor, and radar eyes at Tonopah
feeding their GCI. The counters are the ones we paid for in the Route Packs: know where the site
is before you fly there — that's what the recon birds are for — never give a Fan Song steady,
predictable flight in its envelope, beam the launch, make the missile chase a moving target. And
the guns: vary everything, one pass, don't go low to admire your work.

The war escalates on a schedule. **Week One** you fly the corridors — the FEBA, the convoys, the
armor arrays, the SAM sites. The deep targets are off the list, so don't ask. **Week Two** the
list opens — the airfield complex at Tolicha, the depots — and the Red Force answers with a push
against the line, so expect to earn it. **Surge Week**, everything's on the table including their
fields, and we find out what this detachment is made of.

Two more things. There's a scoreboard — call it Washington's patience and their side's will to
keep playing. Every jet we lose, every crew they capture, every rule we break, it moves. Fly
disciplined and it moves our way. And if you come down on those ranges — there are people out
there whose job is to get to you first. Ours are faster. Help them: get to cover, get on the
radio, and let Sandy run it.

Thirteen missions. The aggressors brief your kills at the bar tonight — let's make them brief
ours instead. Step out in ten. Fight's on.
